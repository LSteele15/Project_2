Socio-Economic and COVID-19 indicators in Victoria

By Lachlan Steele
In January 2020, COVID-19 hit Victoria for the first time. Since then, Victoria has endured 3 waves of COVID-19. The most recent wave being the most dangerous and out of control, due to the new wildly infectious Delta variant. The following dashboard investigates 6 different Socio-Economic and COVID-19 indicators (as of October 8th 2021)

These 6 indicators being explored include:
Active Case % of COVID-19,
2nd Vaccination %'s, Median Age,
, % of Non-English Speakers,
Median Weekly Income,
and Population Density.
Here “Non-English Speakers” refers to all people who participated in the 2016 census which considered themselves to speak English “poorly or not at all”.

For our purposes, Victoria will be separated into LGAs (Local Government Areas).
How are active cases of COVID-19 distributed around Victoria? What about vaccination %'s? How much does vaccination really reduce cases?
What areas have the most non-English speakers? What areas are the youngest? What areas are the poorest? What areas are the most dense?
Do these factors relate to where cases are distributed? If so, how? To what extent? What are the key differences between Metropolitan (Metro) and Regional Victoria?

Lets find out...

How do indicators relate to each other?


Below, the relationships between the discussed 6 different indicators are visualised.
Transformations have been added to some of the indicators so the data is more readable, and so relationships converge to a linear trend.
A log transformation was applied to the active cases.
Weekly Income was normalised - so it is expressed in terms of standard deviations below/above the mean.
A square root transformation was applied to population density.
Drag the slider at the bottom to remove areas of lower population.

Click and drag to highlight certain LGAs (points). Click on "Metro" or "Regional" in the top right to highlight a region.

Areas with a higher 2nd vaccination % clearly tend to have fewer cases. This is true for both regional and metro areas. This is clear evidence of the effectiveness of vaccines. On average, regional areas tend to have higher vaccination rates and therefore less cases than metro areas.

Older areas tend to have a higher 2nd vaccination %. This is true for both regional and metro areas. This is unsurprising since vaccines were made eligible to older Australians first. People from regional areas on average are clearly older than those from metro areas. This would explain why regional areas have higher vaccination %'s.

Older areas tend to have lower case rates overall. The most plausible explanation for this is because older areas have higher vaccination rates, which in turn leads to lower case numbers. However, this is only true for metro areas. If we highlight just regional areas, there seems to be little relationship between these indicators.

The % of non-English speakers in regional areas are extremely low compared to metro areas, and do not yield much information. Metro areas with a higher % of non-English speakers tend to be younger, less vaccinated, have higher case numbers, and are lower income areas. There are two metro areas that are outliers with a significantly higher % of non-English speakers.

On average, people from metro areas have a greater income than those from regional areas. The only clear relationship for regional areas involving income, is that lower income regional areas tend to have older populations. However, for metro areas, income has no association with age. There appears to be no other relationships between income and other indicators for regional areas. Higher income metro areas tend to have higher case numbers and a younger population. There are outlier metro areas with significantly higher income than the rest.

Obviously, regional areas have a far smaller population density compared to metro areas. and as a result provide little insight. People from densely populated metro areas tend to have greater income. However, there appears to be no relationship between population density and any other indicator. This is surprising, as population density is known to assist the transmission of COVID-19. A possible explanation is that the other indicators cause a much greater variation in case numbers, meaning the affect of population density is insignificant in comparison.

How strong is the correlation between any two indicators
for Metro Melbourne?

Below is a grid representing the correlations between each indicator.

Here, we use the Pearson Correlation Coefficient (R-value). This measures the linear correlation between any two variables and spans values -1 to 1. An R-value close to 1 implies that variables have a strong positive correlation, whilst a value close to -1 implies that variables have a strong negative correlation. Values close to 0 indicates that variables have no correlation. Magnitudes between 0.25 and 0.50 generally imply weak correlations between variables, whilst magnitudes between 0 and 0.25 generally mean variables are uncorrelated.

Since the R-value measures the linear correlation between variables, if indicators are strongly related in a non-linear fashion, it may result in a low R-value. This is why it was essential in the previous section to transform the indicators to linearity.

The tiles on the diagonal are blank, as they represent the correlation between two identical indicators. Although they have an R-value of 1, they contain no useful information.

This chart only contains the data from metro areas. The reason for this is that often regional areas did not follow a trend, where metro areas did. The only exception to this is for Age vs Income. For Age vs Income, metro areas had nearly no correlation (R-value 0.08), whilst regional areas clearly displayed a negative correlation. Since metro areas contain most of the population and most of the COVID-19 cases, it is sensible to focus there.

Observing the 9 squares in the top-left corner, notice that 2nd Vaccination %, Active Cases, and Age, are all strongly correlated with one another. These 3 are correlated together more so than any other indicators. Moreover, the strongest correlation is between age and 2nd vaccination %, followed by the correlation between 2nd vaccination % and active cases, followed by the correlation between active cases and age. This supports the earlier hypothesis that older areas are what lead to high vaccination rates which leads to fewer active cases.

Population Density is uncorrelated with every indicator exept for Median Income

For each indicator, how do the distributions for
Metro and Regional areas compare?

To obtain 5 point summaries and individual LGA stats, hover the mouse over each boxplot/points.

As shown previously, Regional Victoria have a higher 2nd Vaccination % than Metro Melbourne. Bayside is leading the way in 2nd Vaccination % for Metro Victoria, with a considerably higher % then the other metro areas. Queenscliffe has an outstanding 87% 2nd vaccination percentage. However it should be noted, that Queenscliffe is an extremely small area, spanning only 9km^2 with a population of under 3000 people. Mitchell, Mildura and Latrobe lag behind the rest of Regional Victoria in vaccination %.


With the exception of Mitchell which clearly has the worst Active Case % in Regional Victoria, all regional areas have very low active case %'s. In fact, at least a quarter of all regional areas have 0 cases.
Unsurprisingly, Metro Melbourne has signicantly worse active case %'s' across the board. Hume has by far the worst case %, with over double the next highest area (Whittlesea). These two areas share a considerable bulk of cases.


As shown previously, Regional LGAs have a much higher Median Age compared to Metro LGAs. Regional LGAs have a larger range of median ages than their Metro counterparts. The Mornington Peninsula is the only outlier, with a signficantly older population than the rest of Metro Melbourne.



Regional Victoria has very few Non-English Speakers. Swan Hill and Greater Shepparton are the only areas with over 3% of their population who do not speak English. Metro Melbourne has a much greater range of values. Areas have %'s that range from as low as 1%, to as high as 15%. Greater Dandenong and Brimbank are clear outliers with a significantly higher % of non-English speakers than the rest of Metro Victoria.


Metro LGAs tend to have a higher Median Weekly Income than Regional LGAs. That being said, Metro Melbourne has a much wider range of Median Incomes. Greater Dandenong has a Median Weekly Income of $475, which is towards the lower end of Regional Victoria. Meanwhile, Stonnington, Yarra, and Port Phillip have Median Weekly Incomes of over $1000. Far more than the rest of Metro Melbourne.



Regional Victoria is obviously very sparse compared to Metro Melbourne. All its LGAs have a very low Population Density. Its entire distribution is squished to 0 compared to Metro Melbourne, which contains LGAs with a wide variety of population densities. These extend from sparse areas like Yarra Ranges (231 people/km^2),
to dense areas such as Port Phillip (5504 people/km^2).

Indicators by Metro Melbourne LGAs

Now we will take a closer look at the specific LGAs of Metro Melbourne.
We will see if observing the geography of the LGAs in combination with their indicator values will allow for further insight.
Highlight an LGA by clicking, to compare indicators across that LGA.

Majority of active cases are clustered in the north-east of Melbourne , with Hume being the clear COVID-19 hotspot. It makes sense that neighbours of Hume also have high case %'s. This includes the second highest hotspot - Whittlesea. There is also some spreading in the South East, including Greater Dandenong, Casey and Cardinia.

The most densely populated area is Port Phillip. Starting from that point, population density reduces as you move radially outward, exhibited by a smooth gradient on the map.

Greater Dandenong and Brimbank both have extremely high percentages of non-English speakers. Outer areas on the east side such as Nillumbik, Yarra Ranges, Cardinia, and Mornington Peninsula also have very low percentages.

Notice the similarity pattern between 2nd Vaccination % and Active Cases. There are much lower vaccination rates in the north west and south east - which mirrors the distrubtion of active cases.

The map of Median Age, also looks similar to the map of 2nd Vaccination % and Active Cases. This supports the correlation findings from earlier.

Areas around the CBD such as Port Phillip, Stonnington, and Yarra are unsurprisingly home to people with the largest Median Weekly Income. Also, areas of low Median Income seem to match the areas of high % of non-English speakers. Particularly Greater Dandenong and Brimbank.

How do LGA's compare across different indicators?

The following chart ranks each LGA for each indicator. The lengths of the bars quantifies the indicator for each LGA. Notice that for all indicators, metro and regional LGAs appear clustered on either end of each scale.
Hover the mouse over a bar to identify the LGA and its indicator numbers. Select an LGA by clicking any bar with the mouse. You can do this for multiple bars. Using this, we can see where any given LGA ranks for all 6 indicators.
For instance, Hume ranks 1st for Active Case %, towards the top for non-English speaking %, towards the bottom for Vaccination %, Median Age, and Weekly Income, and towards the center for population density.

You can also select a range of bars by clicking and dragging on any chart . This way you can get a hands on feel for all the discoveries made in the previous sections.
For instance, if we highlight all the LGAs on the lower end of the Median Age scale, we can directly see it corresponds with lots of LGAs on the lower end of 2nd Vaccination % scale, and the higher end of Active Cases per 100,000 scale.
